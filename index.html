Event Ops Playbook

Event planning today is an art that can easily be a frustrating experience fraught with short deadlines, ephemeral resources, poor timing and miscalculations. To increase the success rate of these projects, we've created a new approach called EventOps short for Event Operations. Drawing from best practices of event planning, battle hardened by the incessant firefighting and honed in the ancient techniques of Service Design (SD), Design Thinking (DT), Human Centered Design (HCD) while combining best practices from Agile, PeopleOps and DevOps. We've created a playbook of 11 key “plays” to enhance event flow. Flow is defined as a "mental state of operation in which a person performing an activity is fully immersed in a feeling of energized focus, full involvement, and enjoyment in the process of the activity”. Using this playbook will help you build event experiences that will keep people coming back for more. An introductory essay on DT/HCD/UX can be found here: https://medium.com/digital-experience-design/how-to-apply-a-design-thinking-hcd-ux-or-any-creative-process-from-scratch-b8786efbf812#.v6nv1xqmd We were heavily influenced by the DT tools called the double diamond layered with Agile.

    The Double Diamond is a structured design thinking approach to tackle challenges in four phases:
  1. Discover /Research— insight into the problem (diverging)
  2. Define/Synthesis — the area to focus upon (converging)
  3. Develop/ Ideation— potential solutions (diverging)
  4. Deliver /Implementation— solutions that work (converging)

The agile manifesto lists four core values:

  1. Individuals and interactions over processes and tools
  2. Working software over comprehensive documentation
  3. Customer collaboration over contract negotiation
  4. Responding to change over following a plan

PLAY 1

Take a design first approach

Whenever you create an event, you are creating a product which is an experience. We need to focus on experiences to better events. To embrace a layered experience we need to start with Human Centered Design (HCD), Design Thinking (DT) and Service Design (SD). We start with why, become the who before we build the what. In EventOps we look to build events in “stacks” or purposeful experiences to ensure we are can align stakeholders, participants and organizers expectations. Implementing a kinesthetic borrows from the 6 Principles of Design(): “Unity/Harmony, Balance, Hierarchy, Scale/Proportion, Dominance/Emphasis, Similarity & Contrast”. Design Once, Run Anywhere. Key Questions

Based on d.school Design Thinking bootcamp:
  • We ask ourselves four basic questions about our Point of View:
    • What's the point? – What is your team's angle?
      • What is your team's framework in stating a POV?
      • Is it User-centered, Need-based, and Insight-driven?
    • Who says? – How valid is your team's POV?
      • Is your position supported by findings from users?
      • Is it a distillation of findings?
      • Is this applicable outside of one colorful interview?
      • What's new? – What is the value-add of your POV?
        • Have you articulated your findings in a new way?
        • Are they placed in the context of a user?
        • If your POV doesn’t feel new, try being more specific.
      • Who cares? – How is your POV significant?
        • Your team should be excited at this point!
        • Is this work worth doing? If not, ask yourself why?

Reframe/rephrase until you get it right.
PLAY 2

Focus on the team experience

Putting people together is a growth experience in itself but becoming a team is a journey. Building teams that pursue the joys of high performance can only be done by breaking down the barriers and anti-patterns between one another. Every impression builds on the next and becomes part of towards their goal.

Checklist

Some points borrowed from USDS playbook
  1. Do you have Creatives, Builders and Connectors?
  2. Identify pain points in the current way users interact with the service, and prioritize these according to user needs
  3. Design the digital parts of the service so that they are integrated with the offline touch points people use to interact with the service
  4. Develop metrics that will measure how well the service is meeting user needs at each step of the service

Key Questions

  • Is the team T-shaped? Cross-functional? Multi-disciplined?
  • Do they have ownership and autonomy? Or a sense of it?
  • Are they aligned to a common purpose?

PLAY 3

Don't go it alone

Partnerships are key to any long term success and are either of internal or external nature. Internal partnerships are focused on common alliances and goals with in an organization between different business function. External partnerships look towards the community, public and private sector with a focused size and scope and outcomes. The diversity of partnerships can introduce flexibility, feasibility and harmony that may otherwise be too stressful for one organization or group to bear. Finding common goals and forming alliances can speedup and increase impact of event outcomes.

Checklist

  1. Find the coalition of the willing
  2. Use the design style guide consistently for related digital services
  3. Use a list of common goals
  4. Use design principles to maximize beneficial scenarios
  5. Ensure transparency between groups
  6. Share the load
  7. Use language and design consistently throughout the service, including online and offline touch points

Key Questions

  • Who are potential partners?
  • Do they align with your organizational values?
  • What do they offer in return?
  • How can synergy be maximized?
  • Where will the largest support come from?
PLAY 4

Break down silos

The highest priority in Lean-agile thinking is to satisfy the customer through early and continuous delivery of value. In EventOps we emphasize increased quality and frequency of feedback. We reduce the time between planning and execution to limit work in progress. We improve repeatability of planning events. Building an event pipeline allows us to use best practices from the software industry, showcasing aspects of visibility, feedback and control. Using a service design phase gate of pre, intra and post we can construct stages of event strategy to execution.

Checklist

  1. Are your event stages properly defined from inception to conception?
  2. Transparency in place?
  3. WIP limits defined?
  4. Is flow or value production being measured as a means of productivity? ?
  5. Release features and improvements multiple times each month

Key Questions

  • What stages exist end to end for each layer of the event? (stakeholders, organizers, customer)
  • Are the stacks visible?
  • Can you see the WIP (Work in Progress)?
PLAY 5

Embrace the wisdom of the crowd

The only way to find a current perspective on a topic or a narrative is to ask. Crowdsourcing is a quick way of understanding a situational context, common themes, sources of pain etc. Crowds are also known for their heterogeneity and diversity that decrease groupthink. Tapping into the wisdom of the many can accelerate efforts by finding new avenues to explore. Ensuring that experts also exist in the crowd can help facilitate quality solutions. On a scale from 1 to 5 rate the effectivness of your crowd-sourcing: Diversity Measurable Clear and direct questions Timely

Checklist

  1. What kind of information are you seeking to gain more insight about?
  2. How can you use DT and facilitation to better guide the crowd?
  3. How can you make sure experts help shape input?

Key Questions

  • What is the scope of the project? What are the key deliverables?
  • What are the milestones? How frequent are they?
  • What are the performance metrics defined in the contract (e.g., response time, system uptime, time period to address priority issues)?

Be agile

PLAY 10

Know when to pull the cord

"The origin of the word “Andon” in Japanese comes from the use of traditional lighting equipment using a fire burning lamp made out of paper and bamboo. This “Andon” idea was later translated for use in manufacturing in Japan. The “Andon” became used as a signal to highlight an anomaly (i.e., a flashing light). This signal would be used to amplify potential defects in quality. When a defect was suspected, a sign board would light up signaling the specific workstation having a problem. The signal event would also indicate that the system was stopped due to the defect and was waiting for the problem to be resolved. The process of stopping a system when a defect was suspected originates back to the original Toyota System Corporation to something called Jidoka. The idea behind Jidoka is that by stopping the system you get an immediate opportunity for improvement, or find a root cause, as opposed to letting the defect move further down the line and be unresolved. The Jidoka concept was pioneered by the original Toyota founder, Sakichi Toyoda. Sakichi Toyoda is known as the father of the Japanese industrial revolution and also the founder of the original Toyota Systems Corporation (before they manufactured automobiles). Sakichi Toyoda invented the automatic power loom in 1924 where the loom would automatically stop if a sewing needle broke. Before his invention, the loom would continue even when the needle was broken. When this occurred, the downstream process would produce runs in the fabric stitches of the final product. " (Andon Cord - http://itrevolution.com/kata/)
